The Future of AI in the World

March 11, 2026
Future Technology Society

These past two weeks I've been hearing about AI scientist Yann LeCun's work through peers, social media and more — so I decided I must research what's going on. I was vaguely familiar with the "Godfather of AI" and Turing Award winner, as he was one of Meta's AI researchers until Mark Zuckerberg turned that upside down in 2025. LeCun has been one of the biggest critics of Large Language Models, and I highly agree with his argument that LLMs are not the final answer to AI development — that they are in fact high-end pattern recognizers and matchers able to predict the next word.

This is not real intelligence, but in fact a highly efficient database of existing information and knowledge.

LeCun is now working at Advanced Machine Intelligence (AMI), a European company building "world models" — systems trained on video and spatial data that can retain memory, reason and plan complex actions, with potential applications in robotics and transport. This is being done through an AI framework called Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture (JEPA), a non-generative AI framework designed by LeCun during his time at Meta. Essentially, LeCun states we must train artificial intelligence through real-world interactions — not just text — to achieve true human-level intelligence.

This got me thinking about the main questions here: what is the maximum intelligence, effects and reach of Large Language Models? And what are the primary differences between World Models versus Language Learning Models?

World Models are essentially internal simulations of reality, utilizing physics, intentions, memory, perception and more to understand the workings of our world. All of these aspects build up to the ability to accurately predict what would happen in a given scenario. This differs largely from the LLMs we're already familiar with, which are extremely advanced text pattern recognition machines. LLMs do not look at context and logic in the way humans do — rather, they solely predict the next word based on patterns and probabilities seen in text-based contexts previously.

LLMs have large rates of hallucinating and falsifying data, which makes me curious to see what this challenge would look like in the context of world models. If a world model is simulating physical reality, the stakes of a "hallucination" become entirely different — and arguably much higher. A misread of spatial context or physical causality isn't just a wrong answer; it could mean a robot misnavigating, or an autonomous system making a dangerous decision.

What excites me most is that this shift — from language to world — signals that we are still in the very early chapters of what AI can become. The LLM era gave us remarkable tools, but LeCun's work suggests that the next frontier isn't about making pattern-matching faster or bigger. It's about building systems that actually understand the world, the way a child learns by touching, falling, watching and doing. That is a fundamentally different project — and a far more ambitious one.
