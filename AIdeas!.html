AIdeas

A running list of places where I think AI could make life easier, safer, or more thoughtful. I'm constantly noticing gaps in everyday systems and thinking about how smarter design could fill them.

  • Airline seating that optionally takes passenger height, weight, and preferred seat neighbor gender into account during random seat assignment. This came to me when a flight attendant had to manually move my petite friend who had been seated between two grown men. A small data input at booking could automate this with dignity and without any mandatory disclosure.
  • Lab result readers that explain what is flagged, what it means, and what questions to bring to your doctor in plain language.
  • Medication conflict checkers that run in real time as prescriptions are added to a patient profile.
  • Mental health check-ins that live inside apps people already use daily and surface patterns over time without requiring the user to seek anything out.
  • Personalized tutors that adapt to how a student actually processes information, not just how fast they move through content.
  • A short quiz at the start of any learning tool that identifies how a student best absorbs new material, so the system can adjust its approach from the beginning rather than calibrating over time.
  • Writing feedback tools that explain why a revision improves the work rather than just making the change for you.
  • Navigation tools for visually impaired users that describe environments conversationally rather than with rigid turn-by-turn instructions.
  • Grocery planning that accounts for what is already in your fridge, your dietary goals, and current sales. Stemmed from an AI/ML study I was part of.
  • Plain-language summaries of ballot measures written directly from official legislative text with no editorial framing. It took me an embarrassingly long time to understand the electoral college.
  • Tools that help people navigate bureaucratic processes like benefits applications or permit appeals that are currently inaccessible due to complexity.
  • Carbon footprint context shown at the point of purchase so the environmental impact of a choice is visible in the moment it is made.
